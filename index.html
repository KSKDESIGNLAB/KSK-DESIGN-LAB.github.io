PHILOSOPHY

Cars as mechanical art, not digital appliances.

Modern cars hide their engines under plastic, bury their character behind screens and drive modes, and chase numbers instead of feel. KSKWERK goes the other way: reveal the engine, simplify the interfaces, and let the materials, sound, and vibration speak.

  • Inline-six and V12 engines with visible cam covers, ITBs, and equal-length headers.
  • No plastic engine covers. No fake vents. No unnecessary screens.
  • Factory body lines remain intact – no widebody kits, no loud graphics.
  • Interiors finished like fine leather goods, not consumer electronics.
  • Tuned for feel: steering, throttle, and the way the car breathes on a winding road.
QUAD EYES ENGINE PROGRAM

M20 / M30 / M70(S70) reimagined as mechanical sculpture.

The heart of KSKWERK is the QuadEyes Engine concept – BMW’s M20, M30 and M70-based V12 rebuilt with modern precision and a focus on visual harmony. These are engines meant to be seen with the bonnet open, not hidden away.

QUADEYES M20

Heritage Lightweight Six

Applications: 2002 / E30 Cabriolet

A compact straight-six tuned for response and sound. Individual throttle bodies with a period-correct look, equal-length headers, modern internals, and careful balancing. Built to deliver that unmistakable classic BMW M20 character – updated with contemporary precision.

QUADEYES M30

Grand Touring Six

Applications: E9 / E24

BMW’s big straight-six, reworked as a centerpiece for E9 and E24 GT builds. ITBs, a cleaned and symmetrical engine bay layout, and an exhaust tuned for a deep, cultured note. Designed for long, fast journeys rather than pure track use – a true grand touring heart.

QUADEYES M70(S70)

Deep Resonance V12

Application: E31

A V12 built for low frequency presence rather than high-pitched scream. M70-based, inspired by S70 thinking: ITB conversion, dry sump system, enlarged cooling, and a titanium exhaust. The goal is a V12 that looks like an old racing engine and idles like distant thunder.

Each QuadEyes engine is conceived first as a visual and acoustic object, then as a mechanical package. Power and torque are important – but never at the expense of feel or aesthetics.

FROM JAPAN

German heritage, Japanese craftsmanship.

Japan is not known for BMW restomods, but Japanese craftsmanship is respected worldwide – in metalwork, paint, and leather. KSKWERK aims to combine German mechanical heritage with Japanese precision and quiet obsession.

  • Welding and fabrication focused on strength and cleanliness, not just speed.
  • Paintwork and corrosion protection designed for long-term survival, not show-only use.
  • Interior stitching and materials inspired by high-end leather goods rather than mass production.
  • A cultural preference for refinement and restraint – no unnecessary noise or drama.
ORIGIN STORY

A childhood E30, an unfinished car, and a decision to start over.

KSKWERK began with a sound: the straight-six of a BMW 320i returning home in Japan. As a child, the M20’s note and the smell of the cabin were part of the background – not yet “passion”, but a quiet imprint.

Years later, an Inka Orange E30 Cabriolet with white leather appeared. It became an obsession: collecting parts, dreaming of Alpina-level craftsmanship, planning a build that would combine mechanical beauty and a refined interior far beyond factory level.

The car was sold before that dream was realised. The parts were never installed. For a time, it felt like the end of the story – and even a loss of interest in cars themselves.

KSKWERK is the decision to finish that story properly – not by recreating the exact same car, but by building the kind of BMWs that should have existed in the first place.

  • An E30 Cabriolet elevated beyond Alpina, with an M20 shown like an art piece.
  • An E9 and E24 that feel like mechanical sculptures rather than old cars.
  • An E31 whose V12 looks and sounds like a vintage racing engine.
FUTURE & CONTACT

From a personal dream to a focused atelier.

At this stage, KSKWERK is a one-person vision from Japan – no factory, no marketing department, just an obsession with classic BMWs and mechanical beauty. The next steps are simple: build a prototype, refine it, and then quietly open the door to a small number of like-minded owners.
